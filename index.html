01 — Disease Course

Three stages, one continuous thread

Alzheimer's doesn't arrive all at once. It moves gradually along a continuum — recognizing where someone is helps families know what kind of support to offer next.

Early Stage

Mild

Misplacing items, trouble finding words, difficulty planning or organizing. Often mistaken for normal aging. Most people are still independent and aware something is changing.

Middle Stage

Moderate

The longest stage, often lasting years. Confusion about time and place, difficulty recognizing family, changes in mood or behavior, and need for daily care assistance increase.

Late Stage

Severe

Loss of ability to communicate verbally, full-time care needs, and physical decline. Emotional connection through touch, voice, and music often remains possible.

02 — Early Detection

Normal aging vs. warning signs

Some forgetfulness is a normal part of getting older. Alzheimer's looks different — it disrupts daily life, not just the occasional memory slip.

Typical age-related changes

  • Sometimes forgetting names, but recalling them later
  • Occasionally misplacing items, but retracing steps to find them
  • Needing help with new technology, not familiar routines
  • Making a wrong decision once in a while

Possible warning signs

  • Forgetting recently learned information, asking the same questions repeatedly
  • Losing track of dates, seasons, or how time is passing
  • Trouble completing familiar tasks, like managing a budget or following a recipe
  • Withdrawing from work, hobbies, or social activities
03 — Risk & Resilience

What the research says about brain health

No single habit prevents Alzheimer's, but a 2015 Finnish trial — FINGER — found that combining several lifestyle changes measurably slowed cognitive decline in at-risk adults.

Move daily

Regular aerobic exercise improves blood flow to the brain and was a core pillar of the FINGER protocol's combined intervention.

Ngandu et al., The Lancet, 2015

Eat for vascular health

Diets emphasizing vegetables, fish, and limited saturated fat — similar to Mediterranean patterns — were linked to slower cognitive decline.

Morris et al., Alzheimer's & Dementia, 2015

Keep the mind active

Cognitive training — puzzles, learning new skills, structured mental exercises — was directly tested as one of FINGER's four intervention arms.

Ngandu et al., The Lancet, 2015

Stay socially connected

Social isolation is independently associated with higher dementia risk; regular meaningful contact appears protective.

Livingston et al., The Lancet Commission, 2020
04 — For Families

Caregivers carry this too

Alzheimer's reshapes the caregiver's life as much as the patient's. Burnout, grief, and isolation are common — and treatable as part of the care plan, not an afterthought.

You don't have to carry this alone.

06 — Volunteer

Volunteer with NeuroNex

Join a growing movement of students, families, and professionals dedicated to reducing Alzheimer's isolation, empowering caregivers, and translating science for the communities that need it most.

What volunteers do

  • Lead community Alzheimer's awareness workshops and events
  • Support caregivers through companionship visits and check-in calls
  • Help families navigate resources and local services
  • Contribute to NeuroNex content, research translation, and outreach
  • Help curate and expand the NeuroNex Research Database by summarizing new Alzheimer's findings in plain language
  • Assist with app testing, technical development, or design
  • Represent NeuroNex at health fairs and school presentations
All activities are coordinated to ensure a meaningful, safe, and impactful experience for both volunteers and the families we serve.

Who can volunteer?

  • Middle school, high school, and college students
  • Parents and adults who want to contribute their time
  • Teachers and school clubs looking for service projects
  • Healthcare professionals and researchers
  • Anyone passionate about Alzheimer's awareness and community care

Why volunteer?

  • Reduce isolation for patients and caregivers
  • Build empathy, leadership, and research literacy
  • Earn verified volunteer hours for school and college applications
  • Make real, lasting connections in your community
  • Be part of a student-led national awareness movement
